Life's molecular machines have been shaped by billions years of evolution. While these macromolecular machines have conserved components suggesting they evolved for optimal efficiency, they nonetheless are rampent with unintuitive mechanism by which they function and evolve. My research aims to gain a better understanding of the natural world within, and around us using the historical context of evolution. In particular I am fascinated by the process of chromosome segregation and the processes that ensure its high fidelity across eons. My long term ambition to gain a high resolution understanding of how genomes functionally evolve. To do this I am using the diversity of an entire subphylum of species, the Saccharomycotina, which is home to the Brewer’s yeast Saccharomyces. The benefits of this systems are many: 1) They are closely related to humans and share thousands of genes and functions, 2) They are as diverse at the level of DNA as all animals and plants, 3) They have small, simplified genomes, and species-specific genetic manipulation are possible, and 4) Their small genomes have undergone many revolutionary changes, making their genomes a treasure trove of compelling evolutionary stories.

My goal is to achieve a phylum level resolution of functional genome elements like the centromeres to better understand how species and genomes evolve. These question manifest at many levels, but my current focus is on the precise atomic structure of the native centromere-kinetochore complex and the evolutionary derivation of point centromeres.

Research

Current Research

My current research explores how Brewer's yeast centromeres evolved and how their centromeres nucleate kinetochore assembly. I use multidisciplinary approaches such as phylogenetics, genomics, biochemistry, and structural methods.

Centromere Evolutionary Genomics

Unlike the majority of eukaryotes, Brewer's yeast have genetic "point" centromeres. Despite being the first centromeres to be discovered over forty years ago, we still have no idea how they evolved. As they shared no clear homology to other centromere types, piecing togther this puzzle has not been possible. Our work in a sister group to Brewer's yeast revealed a new type of centromere, "proto-points", which represent an evolutionary transistion state that showed that point centromeres are direct decendents of LTR retrotransposons.

  • Haase MAB@, Lazar-Stefanita L, Baudry L, Wudzinska A, Zhou X, Rokas A, Hittinger CT, Musacchio A, Boeke JD@. (2025). Ancient co-option of LTR retrotransposons as yeast centromeres. bioRxiv, 2025.04.25.647736.

I am also interested in how historical contigency shapes modern day centromere function. From this perspecitive I am seeking to understand how the ancient allopolyploid whole genome duplication event shaped modern day function of S. cerevisiae's' centromeres. The original observation and hypothesis were published in my PhD work.

  • Haase MAB, Ólafsson G, Flores RL, Boakye-Ansah E, Zelter A, Dickinson MS, Lazar-Stefanita L, Truong DM, Asbury CL, Davis TN, Boeke JD@. (2023). DASH/Dam1 complex mutants stabilize ploidy in histone-humanized yeast by weakening kinetochore-microtubule attachments. EMBO Journal

Native Centromere-Kinetochore Structure (CENStruK)

Despite knowing how yeast centromeres evolved, we still lack a detailed molecular understanding of how they nucleate kinetochore assembly. I am now investigating native centromere-kinetochore strucutes to uncover the atomic details of how centromere DNA does this.
